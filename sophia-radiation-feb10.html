Treatment: Focal SRS

Focal stereotactic radiosurgery (SRS) — precisely targeted radiation on individual brain lesions. Chosen over whole brain and craniospinal radiation.

Why focal instead of whole brain? Whole brain radiation requires ~2 weeks + significant downtime during which Sophia can't take systemic therapy (Verzenio/Aromasin). Dr. Yu wants to keep her on systemic treatment without interruption.
  • Focal SRS is the mildest option — better than whole brain AND better than no treatment
  • Systemic therapy can also help shrink brain tumors of this type
  • Treating at 5mm is dramatically safer than waiting for growth
⚠
Critical: Trigeminal Nerve / Brainstem Lesion
Dr. Yu's #1 concern. A tumor is sitting directly in front of the brainstem. The trigeminal nerve root originates in the brainstem. Even slight growth could push into the brainstem, causing life-threatening problems.
  • If untreated and tumor grows: Trouble talking, swallowing, aspiration — life-threatening
  • Radiation side effect risk: Numbness or painful numbness on left side of face
  • NOT paralysis — no facial asymmetry expected
  • For tumors not yet symptomatic, numbness tends to be short-term
  • Treatable with steroids if inflammation occurs
  • This is why Dr. Yu is motivated to treat now — preventing brainstem compression
△
Falx Lesion (Between Hemispheres)
  • Located in connective tissue between the two brain hemispheres — not in brain tissue itself
  • Requires beams from multiple angles at the top of the head
  • Main risk: temporary hair loss at top of head
  • Frontal lobe proximity — things may feel "a little slower" for a couple weeks
  • Young patients generally recover well
📊
Expected Side Effects

What ~90% of patients experience:

  • Mild fatigue
  • Mild headache
  • Both resolve on their own

Short-Term (days to weeks after treatment)

  • Fatigue, headache, possible cognitive slowness
  • If headaches occur → contact Dr. Yu → short steroid taper
  • Possible temporary hair loss (falx lesion)

Radiation Necrosis Risk (long-term)

Inflammation/necrosis at the treatment site. Onset typically 6 months to 1-2 years after treatment.

Dr. Yu's framing: Near 100% chance of serious problems if untreated (months), vs. ~2% necrosis risk at 2 years if treated now. "Probably a pretty good trade-off."

If Necrosis Occurs — Treatment Options

  • Steroids — resolves ~50% of cases
  • Avastin (bevacizumab)
  • Boswellia serrata (frankincense) — surprisingly well-supported by clinical studies. Dr. Yu has prescribed it. No objection to starting now.

Antiparasitic Drug Discussion

Robert asked about mebendazole, ivermectin, and fenbendazole as supplementary treatment.

  • Dr. Yu is not aware of strong data supporting them for brain mets
  • Key point: crossing the BBB does NOT equal effective treatment
  • His bar: 80-90% efficacy in 100-200+ patient studies
  • No known interaction between these drugs and radiation
  • Recommended discussing with Dr. Robson and MSK Integrative Medicine
